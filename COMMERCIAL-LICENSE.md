This is the right answer for most users. It's the *wrong* answer for some.

## When you need a commercial license

You probably need a commercial license if any of these apply:

- You want to embed Trivium in a closed-source SaaS product without releasing your source.
- You want to bundle Trivium into proprietary on-premise software you sell to customers.
- You want to fork Trivium and run it as a competing hosted service without contributing your changes back.
- Your company's policy forbids AGPL-licensed dependencies.

## What a commercial license gives you

A perpetual, non-exclusive license to use, modify, and redistribute Trivium in your products **without the AGPL's source-disclosure requirement**. Pricing is per-organization and depends on your use case.

The commercial license does **not** include:
- Support contracts (available separately).
- Custom feature development (negotiated separately).
- Any warranty beyond what's required by applicable consumer-protection law.

## Contributor agreement

If you contribute code to this repository, your contribution remains under AGPL-3.0. To enable dual-licensing (the open-source community gets AGPL, paying customers get the commercial license), contributors are asked to sign a Contributor License Agreement — see [`CLA.md`](CLA.md). The CLA assigns Lams IT Solutions sufficient rights to relicense contributions.

If you'd rather not sign the CLA, your contribution can still be merged but it'll be carved out of any future commercial license — practically this means the contribution stays AGPL-only and commercial customers don't get that piece. We'll be explicit about this when reviewing the PR.
